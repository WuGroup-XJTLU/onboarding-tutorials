Statistical Mechanics

Statistical mechanics is the bridge from the microscopic world of atoms to the macroscopic thermodynamics we measure. It is also the conceptual foundation for every molecular simulation the group runs — an NVT or NPT run only means something once you know what an ensemble is.

Learning objectives

  • Distinguish microstates from macrostates
  • Know the common ensembles and what each fixes
  • Use the Boltzmann distribution
  • Understand the partition function and free energy
  • Connect all of this to what a simulation samples

Prerequisites

  • Undergraduate thermodynamics / physical chemistry
  • Calculus; a little probability

Estimated time

  • 30–35 hours

Overview

A glass of water is about 1024 molecules. We could never track each one, yet the water has a definite temperature, pressure, and density. Statistical mechanics explains how these few macroscopic quantities emerge from an astronomical number of microscopic configurations — by treating the microscopic state as a probability distribution rather than something we track exactly. Everything the group does with molecular dynamics and Monte Carlo is, underneath, a way of sampling one of these distributions.

Microstates, macrostates & ensembles

A microstate is a complete specification of the system — every particle's position and momentum. A macrostate is the handful of bulk quantities we actually observe (e.g. N, V, T). Enormously many microstates correspond to the same macrostate. An ensemble is the collection of all microstates consistent with a given macrostate, each weighted by its probability.

Microcanonical (NVE)

  • fixed N, V, energy
  • isolated system

Canonical (NVT)

  • fixed N, V, temperature
  • in contact with a heat bath

Grand canonical (μVT)

  • fixed chemical potential, V, T
  • exchanges particles & energy

These are exactly the ensembles you choose between when you set up a simulation, which is why they come first.

The Boltzmann distribution

In the canonical ensemble, the probability of a microstate with energy Ei is

P(E_i) = exp(-E_i / k_B T) / Z

The exponential Boltzmann factor e−E/kBT says low-energy states are more probable, and that the temperature T sets how sharply probability falls off with energy. At low T the system huddles in its lowest states; at high T many states become accessible.

The partition function

The normalising sum in the denominator is the partition function:

Z = Σ_i exp(-E_i / k_B T)          (sum over all microstates)

It looks like mere bookkeeping, but Z is the central object of the subject: essentially every equilibrium thermodynamic quantity can be obtained from it by differentiation — average energy, heat capacity, pressure, entropy. If you can compute Z, you know the thermodynamics.

Free energy

The bridge from Z to thermodynamics is the Helmholtz free energy:

F = -k_B T ln Z

At fixed N, V, T, systems evolve toward minimum F — a balance between low energy and high entropy (F = U − TS). Free-energy differences are what determine whether a process happens: binding, mixing, folding, adsorption. Much of computational soft-matter research is, in the end, a fight to compute a free-energy difference accurately.

Speeds & equipartition

For an ideal gas the Boltzmann distribution over momenta gives the Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution of molecular speeds, and the equipartition theorem assigns ½kBT of energy to each quadratic degree of freedom. This is why a thermostat can set temperature by controlling the average kinetic energy of the atoms.

Why this matters for simulation

A molecular-dynamics trajectory or a Monte-Carlo walk is a machine for drawing samples from one of these ensembles. A thermostat couples the system to a heat bath to target the canonical ensemble; a time (or ensemble) average over the sampled microstates estimates the thermodynamic average — provided the run actually explores the relevant states (ergodicity). Keep this picture in mind going into Module 11: the algorithms there are concrete ways of sampling the distributions defined here.

Resources

  • Pathria & Beale, Statistical Mechanics — a standard, thorough graduate text.
  • Blundell & Blundell, Concepts in Thermal Physics — an accessible route in if the above is heavy going.
  • Hansen & McDonald, Theory of Simple Liquids — advanced but self-contained; a superb reference for the classical statistical mechanics behind liquids and simulation (a group favourite).
  • MIT OCW 8.333 — Statistical Mechanics of Particles (M. Kardar). Full lecture notes, problems, and videos, free. ocw.mit.edu · 8.333

Exercise / Deliverable

Work by hand through a two-level system (energies 0 and ε): write down Z, the level populations, and the average energy as functions of T, and sketch how each behaves from low to high temperature. Explain in a sentence what happens as T → 0 and T → ∞.

Optional (a plus)

Write a short Python script that computes the partition function of the 1D Ising model for N spins by direct enumeration, and plot the average energy vs. temperature (the numpy + matplotlib workflow from Module 04). This is good practice for connecting the formalism to code — but it is a bonus, not required.
