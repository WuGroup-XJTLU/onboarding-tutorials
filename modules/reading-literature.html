Reading Academic Literature Effectively

You will read more papers in a PhD than you can possibly read word-for-word. Reading well means triaging fast, extracting what matters, and taking notes you will actually reuse a year later.

Learning objectives

  • Find relevant papers efficiently
  • Triage a paper in a few minutes
  • Read actively with the three-pass method
  • Keep structured, searchable notes

Prerequisites

  • None — start any time

Estimated time

  • 10–15 hours

Overview

Reading is the single most frequent activity in research, and most people are never taught to do it deliberately. The goal is not to read everything — it is to decide quickly what deserves your attention, get the most out of the papers that do, and remember it. A good reading habit compounds: the notes you take this month make next month's reading faster.

Finding papers

  • Google Scholar — broad search; follow the "Cited by" and "Related articles" links to walk outward from a key paper, and set up alerts for topics or authors.
  • Web of Science / Scopus — curated indexes with citation tracking (via your XJTLU library login), good for systematic searching.
  • arXiv and ChemRxiv — preprints, often months ahead of publication in physics/ML and chemistry.
  • Follow the field. Track a few key groups and authors; their recent papers and reference lists are a map of what matters.

The three-pass method

Rather than reading start-to-finish, read in three increasingly deep passes (after S. Keshav). Most papers only ever get pass 1 from you — and that is fine.

  • Pass 1 (~5 min): read the title, abstract, section headings, figures, and conclusion. Decide: is this relevant, and is it credible? Most papers stop here.
  • Pass 2 (~1 hour): read carefully but skim heavy derivations. Understand the contribution, the method, the evidence, and the figures. You should be able to summarise it to a labmate.
  • Pass 3 (several hours): for the handful of papers your work builds on — reconstruct the results, check the assumptions, and note what you would do differently.

Active reading

Read with questions in mind rather than passively highlighting. For any paper worth a second pass, be able to answer: What problem does it solve? What is the key idea? What did they actually do? What are the assumptions and limitations? How does it relate to my work? Mark the parts you don't understand — those are exactly what to ask about in group meeting.

Keeping notes you'll reuse

A note you can't find later is a note you didn't take. Keep short, structured, searchable notes — plain text or Markdown works well and stays portable. A simple per-paper template:

@Author_Year  "Short title"
  Citation : full reference (+ DOI); keep a BibTeX entry for LaTeX
  One line : what it is, in your own words
  Key idea : the main contribution
  Method   : how they did it
  Findings : the results that matter to you
  Relevance: why it matters for my project
  Questions: what's unclear / to follow up

Keep the BibTeX entry for each paper alongside the note so writing the reference list later is trivial. Whether you organise these in files or a citation manager, the discipline — one structured note per paper — is what pays off.

Resources

  • "How to Read a Paper" — S. Keshav. The three-pass method, four pages, worth reading twice. Stanford PDF
  • "Ten simple rules for reading a scientific paper" — Carey et al., PLOS Comput. Biol. 2020. doi.org/10.1371/journal.pcbi.1008032
  • Google Scholar — search, "cited by", and alerts. scholar.google.com
  • arXiv · ChemRxiv — preprint servers. arxiv.org · chemrxiv.org

Exercise / Deliverable

Choose three papers relevant to your intended project. Then:

  1. Apply Pass 1 to all three and write one sentence each on whether (and why) they're relevant.
  2. Give the most relevant one a full Pass 2 and write a structured note using the template above.
  3. Write a short paragraph explaining how the three papers relate to one another and to your project.

Deliverable: the three structured notes (with BibTeX entries) plus the synthesis paragraph.
