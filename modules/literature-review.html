Doing a Literature Review

A literature review is not "reading a lot of papers" — it is a systematic, reproducible way to map what is known about a question, find the gaps, and position your own work against them.

Learning objectives

  • Frame a focused review question
  • Build and document a reproducible search
  • Screen papers against explicit criteria
  • Synthesise findings and identify gaps
  • Report the process with a PRISMA-style flow

Prerequisites

  • Module 12 — Reading Academic Literature

Estimated time

  • 20–25 hours

Overview

Module 12 was about reading individual papers well. A literature review zooms out: it asks what does the field collectively know about X, and what doesn't it? The defining feature is that it is systematic and reproducible — someone else following your recorded method would find the same papers and reach the same picture. For onboarding you will do a small scoping review: a focused, documented survey rather than an exhaustive systematic review.

1 · Scope the question

Start narrow. "Machine-learning force fields" is a field; "How well do message-passing neural-network potentials reproduce polymer glass-transition temperatures?" is a reviewable question. Write down explicitly what is in scope and what is out — years, systems, methods, languages — before you search.

2 · Build a documented search

Choose your databases (Google Scholar for reach; Web of Science / Scopus for curated, citation-tracked results). Then build a search string from your key concepts, their synonyms, and Boolean operators — and record it verbatim, with the date and database, so the search is reproducible.

("machine learning" OR "neural network") AND
  ("force field" OR "interatomic potential") AND
  (polymer OR macromolecul*) AND
  ("glass transition" OR Tg)

Then snowball: from a few core papers, chase citations backward (their reference lists) and forward (who cited them) to catch what keyword search misses.

3 · Screen systematically

Apply explicit inclusion/exclusion criteria in two stages: first on titles and abstracts, then on full text. Record how many papers you drop at each stage and why — those numbers are your PRISMA flow.

A screening tool such as Rayyan helps manage this when there are many records or more than one reviewer.

4 · Map the citation landscape

Tools like Connected Papers build a visual graph of a topic's key works and how they relate — a fast way to check you haven't missed a seminal paper and to see how sub-topics cluster.

5 · Synthesise

Don't summarise papers one by one — organise by theme or method and compare across them. A synthesis table makes patterns and gaps visible:

| Paper        | System      | Method     | Key finding        | Limitation      |
|--------------|-------------|------------|--------------------|-----------------|
| Author 2023  | PE melt     | MACE NNP   | Tg within 5 K      | one chemistry   |
| Author 2024  | PS, PMMA    | SchNet     | systematic offset  | small training  |

The empty cells and repeated limitations point to the gap — which is where your project comes in.

Resources

  • PRISMA 2020 statement — Page et al., BMJ 2021. The reporting standard and flow diagram. doi.org/10.1136/bmj.n71 · prisma-statement.org
  • "Ten simple rules for writing a literature review" — Pautasso, PLOS Comput. Biol. 2013. doi.org/10.1371/journal.pcbi.1003149
  • Connected Papers — visual citation-neighbourhood explorer. connectedpapers.com
  • Rayyan — collaborative screening for reviews. rayyan.ai

Exercise / Deliverable

Do a mini scoping review on a narrow question relevant to your project:

  1. Write the focused question with explicit inclusion/exclusion criteria.
  2. Record your search string(s), database(s), and date; report how many records each returned.
  3. Screen to a small included set and draw a PRISMA-style flow diagram with the counts.
  4. Build a synthesis table of the included papers and write one paragraph naming the gap your project addresses.

Deliverable: the documented search, the PRISMA flow, the synthesis table, and the gap paragraph.
