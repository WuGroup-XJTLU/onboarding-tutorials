Machine Learning for Chemistry & Materials

Machine learning is reshaping molecular and materials science — predicting properties, running simulations at near-quantum accuracy for a fraction of the cost, and proposing new candidate materials. This module maps the landscape and, just as importantly, its pitfalls.

Learning objectives

  • Apply supervised ML to molecular/materials data
  • Choose sensible representations (descriptors, graphs)
  • Understand machine-learned interatomic potentials
  • Query materials databases
  • Recognise where generative models and ML pitfalls apply

Prerequisites

  • Basic Python (numpy, scikit-learn)
  • Helpful: M07, M08

Estimated time

  • 40–45 hours

Overview

Computational chemistry lives with a stubborn trade-off: quantum methods like DFT are accurate but expensive, while classical force fields are cheap but limited. Machine learning attacks this from several directions — learning to predict properties directly, learning interatomic potentials that approach DFT accuracy at simulation cost, and learning to generate new candidate molecules and materials.

1 · Supervised learning, done properly

The basic loop is familiar: featurise your inputs, fit a model (regression for continuous properties, classification for categories), and evaluate it. What bites people in materials science is evaluation:

  • Split honestly. Train / validation / test; tune hyperparameters on validation, report on the untouched test set.
  • Beware data leakage — near-duplicate structures split across train and test inflate scores.
  • Out-of-distribution is the real test. A model that interpolates known chemistries may fail completely on a new one; report where it works and where it doesn't.

2 · Representing molecules & materials

A model is only as good as how you present the structure to it. Good representations respect physical invariances (translation, rotation, permutation of identical atoms). Common choices: fixed descriptors such as SOAP or atom-centred symmetry functions, and graph representations where atoms are nodes and bonds/neighbours are edges (the basis of modern graph neural networks). The representation often matters more than the model.

3 · Machine-learned interatomic potentials

The flagship application in the group's area. A machine-learned potential (MLP / NNP) is trained on energies and forces from DFT and then predicts them for new configurations — letting you run molecular dynamics at near-DFT accuracy but at a cost closer to a classical force field.

Landmark and modern approaches: the Behler–Parrinello high-dimensional NNP, and message-passing / equivariant networks such as SchNet, NequIP and MACE. Their Achilles heel is coverage: a potential is only reliable for configurations resembling its training data.

4 · Data & databases

ML needs data. The Materials Project provides computed properties for hundreds of thousands of materials via a web interface and an API; benchmark suites such as Matbench let you compare methods fairly. The Atomic Simulation Environment (ASE) is the Python glue for reading structures, querying databases, and driving calculators (including ML potentials).

5 · Generative models & inverse design

Rather than screening known materials, generative models — VAEs, GANs, and increasingly diffusion models — propose new candidates with desired properties. The promise is inverse design; the catch is validation: a generated structure is a hypothesis that still has to be checked with simulation or experiment before it means anything.

6 · A word on pitfalls

ML in science amplifies both good and bad practice. Models are confidently wrong outside their training distribution, metrics can flatter through leakage, and a beautiful prediction is still just a prediction. Treat every ML result the way Module 04 treats AI-written code: useful, fast, and to be verified against ground truth before you trust it.

Exercise / Deliverable

  1. Query the Materials Project (via its API / ASE) for a property — e.g. formation energy — across a set of materials, and save a small dataset.
  2. Train a simple regression model on a descriptor to predict that property, with an honest train/test split; report the test MAE and plot predicted vs. true.
  3. (Optional) run a pretrained MACE potential on a small structure to compute its energy and forces, and compare the cost to a DFT single-point.

Deliverable: the dataset, the trained model's parity plot and test error, and a sentence on where you'd expect the model to fail.
