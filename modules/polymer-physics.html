Polymer Physics

Polymers are the group's main subject. What makes them fascinating is that their large-scale behaviour is governed not by chemical detail but by chain connectivity and statistics — a handful of universal scaling laws describe chains of wildly different chemistry.

Learning objectives

  • Describe chain conformations and their scaling with length
  • Distinguish ideal, good- and theta-solvent chains
  • Use Flory–Huggins theory for solution thermodynamics
  • Understand Rouse, Zimm and reptation dynamics

Prerequisites

  • Module 07 — Stat Mech
  • Module 08 — Molecular Simulations

Estimated time

  • 30–35 hours

Overview

A polymer is a long chain of N repeat units. Because N is large, the chain's properties are dominated by statistics rather than by any single bond — which is why polymers of very different chemistry obey the same universal laws. The ideal chain plays the role here that the hydrogen atom plays in quantum mechanics: the solvable model everything else is measured against.

1 · Single-chain conformations & scaling

Model a chain as a random walk of N steps of length b. Its size is measured by the end-to-end distance R or the radius of gyration Rg, and both scale as a power law:

R ~ b · N^ν

The exponent ν encodes the physics:

  • Ideal / Gaussian chain (no self-interaction, or a theta solvent): ν = 1/2. The random-walk result.
  • Real chain in a good solvent (monomers repel — excluded volume): the chain swells, ν ≈ 3/5 (Flory), a self-avoiding walk.
  • Poor solvent: the chain collapses into a globule, ν = 1/3.

2 · Solution thermodynamics: Flory–Huggins

Whether a polymer dissolves, and whether two polymers mix, is captured by Flory–Huggins theory. The free energy of mixing per site is

ΔF_mix / k_B T = (φ/N) ln φ + (1−φ) ln(1−φ) + χ · φ (1−φ)

where φ is the polymer volume fraction and χ is the interaction parameter (roughly, the energetic cost of polymer–solvent contacts). The two logarithmic entropy terms favour mixing, while the χ term can oppose it. The crucial feature is the 1/N: a long polymer has little mixing entropy, so even a small positive χ drives phase separation. This is why polymers are so often immiscible.

3 · Dynamics

How a chain moves depends on length and concentration:

  • Rouse model — an unentangled chain as beads and springs, no hydrodynamics. Predicts relaxation times and a diffusion coefficient scaling as D ~ 1/N. The natural first comparison for coarse-grained melt simulations.
  • Zimm model — adds hydrodynamic interactions; appropriate for dilute solutions, where it changes the scaling.
  • Reptation (de Gennes) — in an entangled melt a chain is confined to a "tube" formed by its neighbours and must snake along its own contour. Predicts the strong slow-down of long-chain melts, e.g. viscosity η ~ N3 (experimentally ~N3.4).

Resources

  • Rubinstein & Colby, Polymer Physics — the group's primary text; the place to learn this material properly.
  • Doi & Edwards, The Theory of Polymer Dynamics — the advanced reference for Rouse/Zimm/reptation.
  • de Gennes, Scaling Concepts in Polymer Physics — the classic on scaling arguments.
  • "Modeling and Simulations of Polymers: A Roadmap" — Macromolecules. Connects the theory to simulation. doi.org/10.1021/acs.macromol.8b01836

Exercise / Deliverable

  1. Generate ideal (unrestricted) and self-avoiding random walks in Python for a range of N; measure R (or Rg) and fit the scaling exponent ν for each. Compare to 1/2 and ~3/5.
  2. Plot the Flory–Huggins ΔFmix vs. φ for several values of χ and identify where the curve develops two minima (phase separation); sketch the resulting phase behaviour as a function of χ.
  3. (Optional, links Module 08) compare a Rouse-model prediction to the mean-squared displacement from a short coarse-grained MD run.

Deliverable: the scaling plot with fitted exponents and the Flory–Huggins free-energy/phase figure.
